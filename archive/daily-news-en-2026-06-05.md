## 🌍 Top 5 Global Stories

### #1 | US House Votes to End Trump's Iran War in Landmark Congressional Rebuke

The US House of Representatives voted 215–208 to invoke the War Powers Act and halt President Trump's war on Iran, marking the first successful congressional pushback since the conflict began on February 28. Four Republicans — Tom Barrett (MI), Warren Davidson (OH), Thomas Massie (KY), and Brian Fitzpatrick (PA) — broke party lines to join Democrats in the historic vote. The US-Israel led war has now entered its fourth month, with thousands of civilian deaths, severe global trade disruptions, and a steep decline in Trump's approval ratings. Secretary of State Marco Rubio also publicly acknowledged that "most of the world assesses" Israel possesses nuclear weapons — a rare US admission. While Trump retains presidential veto power, analysts say the vote fundamentally undermines the administration's negotiating position with Tehran.

**Why it matters:** A sitting US president's war powers have been formally challenged by his own Congress for the first time in decades — reshaping both the Iran conflict and America's domestic political landscape heading into the 2026 midterms.

### #2 | Russia Launches Retaliatory Strikes as Ukraine War Enters Dangerous New Phase

Russia has launched a second wave of systematic strikes against Ukrainian military infrastructure, command posts, and drone-production facilities in retaliation for the May 23 Starobelsk school massacre. That attack — a multiple-wave drone strike by Ukraine on a teacher-training college dormitory in Lugansk — killed 21 people, mostly teenage girls, and injured 70 others. Moscow had warned of "systematic and consistent strikes" in response. Meanwhile, Ukraine launched fresh drone attacks coinciding with the opening of the annual St. Petersburg International Economic Forum (SPIEF), where President Putin is expected to deliver a major address today. Reports from analysts also suggest Belarus may be moving closer to formal entry into the conflict, while Ukraine's frontlines face mounting pressure across multiple sectors.

**Why it matters:** The war's escalation to deliberate strikes on civilian educational facilities and the potential entry of Belarus signals the conflict is expanding rather than de-escalating, despite repeated international mediation efforts.

🏷️ War / Eastern Europe

### #3 | Central Banks Worldwide Brace for Tightening as Inflation Reignites

Eurozone inflation surged to 3.2% in May, driven by accelerating services prices, strengthening the case for a European Central Bank rate hike at its June 11 meeting. Separately, Reuters reported that the Bank of Japan is expected to raise interest rates this month unless a sharp market shock intervenes — a move that would mark a significant shift after decades of ultra-loose monetary policy. Global trade disruptions caused by the Iran war have compounded supply-chain pressures, while energy markets remain volatile. Asian markets are closely watching how coordinated tightening by the ECB, BOJ, and the Fed could ripple through emerging economies and currencies.

**Why it matters:** The world's major central banks are pivoting toward synchronized tightening for the first time in this cycle, threatening to slow global growth just as geopolitical crises multiply.

🏷️ Economy / Markets

### #4 | EU Reshapes Its Strategic Posture: Balkan Enlargement, Hungary Détente, and China Import Talks

The European Union is making a series of significant strategic moves this week. EU leaders are meeting with Western Balkan counterparts to reaffirm membership prospects for the region — a geopolitical counterweight to Russian and Chinese influence. In a notable policy shift, the EU is releasing €16 billion in previously frozen funds to Hungary, a decision that coincides with Budapest allowing the Pride parade to proceed. On the trade front, the EU is discussing potential restrictions on Chinese imports amid growing concerns about overreliance, while the UK is challenging EU plans to almost halve tariff-free steel import quotas. The EU also fined online marketplace Temu for failing to stop the sale of illegal and dangerous products.

**Why it matters:** The EU is simultaneously opening doors (Balkans, Hungary) and building fences (China imports, tech regulation) — a dual-track strategy that will define its global role in the Trump era.

### #5 | Musk-Starmer Clash Erupts Over UK Murder Case; Japan-UK Defense Project at Risk

UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer has publicly accused Elon Musk of "trying to whip up division" in Britain over the Henry Nowak murder case, marking a sharp escalation in tensions between the British government and the world's richest man. Musk has been using his X platform to amplify narratives around the high-profile case, drawing accusations of political interference. Separately, Britain's funding shortfall is threatening the GCAP (Global Combat Air Programme) joint fighter jet project with Japan — a "reality check" for Tokyo, according to the South China Morning Post. The project is central to both nations' Indo-Pacific defense strategies. Meanwhile, Meta is reportedly finalizing a deal with Chinese-founded AI firm Manus to relocate its operations to Singapore, a move seen as an attempt to navigate US-China tech tensions.

**Why it matters:** The convergence of tech billionaires, AI geopolitics, and defense-industrial cooperation illustrates how the boundaries between Silicon Valley, Westminster, and global security have blurred irreversibly.
