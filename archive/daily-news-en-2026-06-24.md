### #1 | Europe Reels Under Record-Shattering Heatwave — France Hits 44.3°C, UK Issues Rare Red Alert

Europe is in the grip of an unprecedented June heatwave, with France recording its hottest day in history at an average of 29.8°C and a peak of 44.3°C in the Landes region. More than half of France remains under a red heat alert, with 58 departments at the maximum warning level; 68,000 homes in Brittany lost power due to transformer failure, and the Louvre museum is closing early through Saturday as its historic building struggles to cope. The UK has issued a rare red heat warning for parts of England and Wales, with over 1,000 schools shutting or closing early and grid operators calling for emergency electricity generation. Italy has placed 16 cities under red alert, the Netherlands has issued Code Orange warnings with 37°C expected, and the heatwave is forecast to spread to Germany, Poland, Croatia, and Hungary through the weekend. A 13-year-old girl drowned in the Seine while trying to cool off, and multiple drowning deaths have been reported across Germany.

**Why it matters:** This is the most intense early-summer heatwave to strike Western Europe in recorded history, testing the continent's infrastructure, energy grids, and public health systems as climate change drives increasingly extreme weather.

### #2 | US Congress Passes Historic War Powers Resolution to Curb Trump's Iran War

In a historic first, both chambers of the US Congress have passed a concurrent resolution demanding that President Donald Trump either halt military operations against Iran or seek congressional approval to continue. The Republican-controlled Senate approved the measure in a 50-48 vote on Tuesday, with four Republican senators — Rand Paul, Lisa Murkowski, Susan Collins, and Bill Cassidy — crossing party lines to join Democrats. This follows the House of Representatives passing the same measure earlier this month in a 215-208 vote. The resolution is largely symbolic and will not be sent to the president for his signature, but it marks the first time since the 1973 War Powers Resolution was enacted that both chambers have instructed a president to end a military action. Trump blasted the vote as "poorly timed and meaningless," while the Pentagon simultaneously requested approximately $80 billion from Congress, most of it to fund the Iran conflict now approaching its fifth month.

**Why it matters:** The bipartisan congressional rebuke signals deep unease over an unpopular and costly war, raising the stakes ahead of November's midterm elections and testing the boundaries of presidential war-making authority.

### #3 | UN to Evacuate 11,000 Sailors Stranded in Strait of Hormuz as Rubio Warns Iran Against Tolls

The UN's International Maritime Organization (IMO) has announced a massive evacuation operation for more than 11,000 sailors stranded in the Gulf since Iran effectively closed the Strait of Hormuz in late February. The IMO says it has secured safety guarantees from Iran, Oman, the US, and other regional states to carry out the rescue via two temporary routes through the strait. US Secretary of State Marco Rubio, beginning a Gulf tour in the UAE with stops in Kuwait and Bahrain, warned bluntly that "no country is allowed to charge tolls or fees on an international waterway," pushing back against Tehran's stated plan to impose maritime service charges. The crisis began on February 28 when US-Israeli strikes on Iran triggered the strait's closure, sending global oil prices beyond $100 per barrel. A ceasefire and memorandum of understanding were signed last week, but the two sides continue to clash over nuclear inspection terms and Iran's ballistic missile stockpile.

**Why it matters:** The Strait of Hormuz handles roughly one-fifth of global oil transit, and the safe reopening of this chokepoint — and the terms under which it operates — will determine energy prices and shipping security for the entire global economy.

### #4 | Ten Years After the Brexit Referendum, the Economic Toll Comes Into Focus

A decade after the UK voted to leave the European Union, a growing body of economic research is quantifying the long-term damage. A new study from Aston University Business School finds a 53.8% loss in the variety of UK goods exported to EU countries and a 31.5% drop in import varieties, while the UK Trade Policy Observatory at Sussex University separately calculated a 26% reduction in export categories by 2023. Stanford professor Nick Bloom, author of a major study using Bank of England data, says the expert consensus is clear: "The experts were right. It was, if anything, worse than we thought, but it's taken longer to get there." The BBC's economics editor profiles firms like Bristol-based Eskimo, whose EU exports collapsed from 40% to just 5% of sales despite the zero-tariff deal — sunk not by duties but by paperwork, delays, and the chilling effect of regulatory divergence.

**Why it matters:** As the first major economy to voluntarily leave a trade bloc in the modern era, the UK's Brexit experience provides a landmark case study on the real-world costs of economic disintegration — lessons that resonate far beyond British shores.

### #5 | Chinese Supercomputer Leapfrogs US Machines to Claim World's Fastest Ranking

A Chinese supercomputer has overtaken the best American systems to be ranked the fastest in the world, according to reports confirmed by The Guardian, marking a significant milestone in the intensifying US-China technology rivalry. The achievement comes amid an escalating tech decoupling between the world's two largest economies, with the US recently adding Chinese e-commerce giant Alibaba to its defence blacklist over alleged military ties — a move Alibaba is now suing to overturn. The breakthrough underscores China's accelerating capabilities in high-performance computing, a domain critical to artificial intelligence, climate modeling, cryptography, and weapons development. It also follows years of US export controls on advanced semiconductor technology designed precisely to slow Chinese advances in supercomputing and AI.

**Why it matters:** Supercomputing supremacy is a proxy for broader technological leadership — whoever builds the fastest machines gains advantages in AI, defense, drug discovery, and climate science, making this ranking a bellwether for the global balance of tech power.
