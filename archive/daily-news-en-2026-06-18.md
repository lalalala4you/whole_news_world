## 🌍 Top 5 Global Stories

### #1 | US and Iran Sign Landmark Agreement to End Hostilities

President Donald Trump and Iranian leaders have signed a 14-point Memorandum of Understanding, now in effect, that declares an "immediate and permanent" end to military operations on all fronts — including Lebanon. The agreement, formalized at the G7 summit in Evian-les-Bains, France, commits Iran to never develop or produce a nuclear weapon and establishes a $300 billion reconstruction and economic development fund for Iran (with no US contribution required). The US has agreed to lift its naval blockade within 30 days, reopening the strategic Strait of Hormuz. However, critics point out that core sticking points remain unresolved, and the $300 billion question — who pays? — looms large. Both sides are expected to sign a final deal within 60 days in Geneva.

**Why it matters:** This agreement reshapes the geopolitical landscape of the Middle East, potentially ending one of the most consequential conflicts of the decade while reopening a chokepoint for ~20% of the world's oil and gas supply.

### #2 | Israel Launches Fresh Strikes on Lebanon Despite Trump Rebuke

Israeli forces have carried out new drone and jet strikes in southern Lebanon, wounding several people in Mansouri, Aaziyyeh, Nabatieh al-Fawqa, and Kfar Tebnit — despite explicit criticism from President Trump, who told Netanyahu to be "more responsible with respect to Lebanon." At the G7, Trump said Israel had been fighting Hezbollah for "too long and too many people are being killed." The US-Iran deal explicitly covers Lebanon, and Iran has warned it would take "necessary measures" if Israeli operations continue. Hezbollah leader Naim Qassem declared the US-Iran agreement a "great victory," while over 3,800 people have been killed in Lebanon since the conflict escalated in March.

**Why it matters:** The Israel-Lebanon front remains the most fragile pillar of the US-Iran deal, and continued hostilities there could unravel the broader ceasefire before the 60-day negotiation window closes.

### #3 | Japan Defence Minister: Military Ramp-Up 'Critical' to Prevent War

Japan's Defence Minister Shinjiro Koizumi told the BBC that strengthening Japan's defence capabilities is essential to preventing war in the region, signaling a historic shift away from the country's post-WWII pacifist constitution. For the first time in ~50 years, Japan can now export lethal weapons to 17 countries. Australia has selected Japanese warships, and talks are underway with the Philippines, Indonesia, and New Zealand. Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi has pushed to revise Article 9 — the constitutional clause renouncing war. Koizumi described China's military moves as the "greatest strategic challenge," while Beijing accuses Japan of "new militarism."

**Why it matters:** Japan's dramatic rearmament — backed by record defence budgets and constitutional reform — marks one of the most significant shifts in Asia-Pacific security dynamics since WWII.

🏷️ Geopolitics / Asia-Pacific

### #4 | Cervical Cancer Deaths Fall to Zero in Young Women Given HPV Vaccine

A landmark study published in The Lancet has found that cervical cancer deaths among women aged 20–24 in England fell to zero between 2020 and 2024 — the first time this has ever been recorded over a five-year period. Without the HPV vaccine, introduced for school-age girls in 2008, around 23 deaths would have been expected. The research estimates that approximately 200 lives have been saved in England so far. Children vaccinated at age 12 or 13 now have close to zero risk of dying from cervical cancer before age 30. However, vaccination rates in England have fallen to 76% — well below the 90% WHO target — raising concerns about future progress.

**Why it matters:** This is the first direct evidence that HPV vaccination is saving lives from cancer, not just preventing pre-cancerous changes — a monumental public health milestone with global implications.

### #5 | Trump Pledges to Visit India as US-India Relations Thaw

President Trump promised to visit India "sometime in the future" during a bilateral meeting with Prime Minister Narendra Modi at the G7 summit, signaling a thaw after months of tension over tariff disputes and the killing of Indian sailors in the Gulf of Oman. The two leaders discussed finalizing a US-India trade deal — with negotiations described as being at the "final touches" stage — and Modi raised the safety of Indian seafarers working in the Strait of Hormuz. Trump called Modi a "tough negotiator" and said the US would "help" India if attacked. Relations had soured after Trump imposed steep tariffs on India and following US strikes that killed Indian crew members aboard oil tankers.

**Why it matters:** India imports ~90% of its oil, and repairing the US-India relationship is critical for both regional security in the Indo-Pacific and global energy stability as the Strait of Hormuz reopens.
