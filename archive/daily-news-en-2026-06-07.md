### #1 | Iran War Reaches 100th Day as US Shoots Down Iranian Drones

The US-Iran war hit its 100-day milestone with the Pentagon confirming it shot down multiple Iranian drones targeting US positions in the Gulf. Mediator Pakistan delivered a direct message to Iran's Supreme Leader, while Trump stated he would "not unfreeze Iran's assets before a peace deal is done." The economic toll is mounting — IATA warned airline profits are expected to halve in 2026 due to a 70% surge in jet fuel prices. Kuwait and Bahrain were also targeted by Iranian retaliatory strikes overnight. Meanwhile, Iran is reportedly grappling with the "perilous prospect of peace," facing domestic blackouts, hyperinflation, and growing dissent as the war grinds on.

**Why it matters:** The Strait of Hormuz remains bottlenecked, energy markets are spiraling, and every extra day of war compounds a global economic shock that now touches everything from aviation to plastics.

### #2 | Russian Drone Hits Chornobyl Nuclear Fuel Facility; Zelensky in London for E3 Talks

A Russian drone struck a spent nuclear fuel storage building near the Chornobyl nuclear plant, which Ukraine's state operator Energoatom called "partially destroyed." A fire was extinguished and radiation levels remain normal, but Zelensky labeled the strike a "vile" deliberate attack on critical nuclear infrastructure. The attack came as Zelensky arrived in London for emergency talks with UK PM Starmer, French President Macron, and German Chancellor Merz — the so-called E3 group — to coordinate European military support. Putin had rejected Zelensky's open letter calling for direct peace negotiations just 24 hours earlier, saying he saw "no point" in meeting the Ukrainian leader.

**Why it matters:** A strike on a nuclear waste facility — even without a radiation leak — crosses a dangerous threshold and underscores the nuclear risks embedded in this four-year war as diplomatic channels remain deadlocked.

🏷️ War / Europe / Nuclear Safety

### #3 | Ebola Outbreak in DR Congo and Uganda on 'Dangerous Trajectory'; Could Match 2014 Record

US CDC modeling warns the Ebola outbreak spreading across DR Congo and Uganda could approach the scale of the devastating 2014 West Africa epidemic that killed over 11,000 people. Rebel attacks in eastern DRC are hampering response efforts, killing at least 30 civilians and disrupting health workers. A controversial US plan to build an "American-only" Ebola quarantine centre in Kenya has drawn sharp criticism from experts who say it undermines coordinated international response. An untreatable strain is complicating containment efforts, and vaccine supplies remain strained.

**Why it matters:** A major Ebola resurgence while global attention and resources are consumed by the Iran war could turn a regional health crisis into an uncontained pandemic.

🏷️ Global Health / Africa

### #4 | UK Deputy PM Tells JD Vance His Comments on Murdered British Teen Were 'Wrong'

UK Deputy Prime Minister David Lammy revealed he called US Vice President JD Vance to directly tell him his remarks about the murder of 18-year-old Henry Nowak were "wrong." Vance had blamed the killing on "the mass invasion of migrants" and European elites' "politics of self-hatred," despite the convicted killer being a British-born citizen. Lammy described the conversation as "robust" but "agreeable," while Nowak's father had urged the public not to exploit his son's death for division. Far-right parties across Europe, including Reform UK, have seized on the case, and the UK's National Police Chiefs' Council is reviewing its anti-racism language in the wake of the murder.

**Why it matters:** A direct diplomatic rebuke from a close ally over race-baiting rhetoric signals deepening friction in the US-UK "special relationship" — already strained by Britain's refusal to join US military action in Iran.

🏷️ Politics / US-UK Relations

### #5 | Hegseth's D-Day Speech on Immigration Condemned as 'Grotesque Stupidity'

US Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth used his D-Day commemoration speech in Normandy to attack European immigration policy, describing migration as a "beach invasion" and linking it to the sacrifice of Allied soldiers. Historians and veterans' groups condemned the remarks as desecrating the memory of those who fell. The speech drew sharp rebukes from European leaders and was labeled "grotesque stupidity" by campaigners. It follows a pattern of Trump administration officials using commemorative events to advance domestic political messaging on migration.

**Why it matters:** Using the D-Day anniversary — one of the most sacred moments in transatlantic memory — to deliver a partisan attack on allies deepens the rift between Washington and European capitals at a moment when coordination on Iran and Ukraine is critical.
